Chocolate Varieties

Four families of chocolate, and what sets each one apart.

Dark chocolate #

Dark chocolate is cocoa mass and cocoa butter with sugar and little or no milk. Bars are labelled by cocoa percentage, usually from about 50% up to 90% or more; the higher the number, the more bitter and less sweet the bar.

Good dark chocolate carries tasting notes the way wine does: red fruit, roasted nuts, tobacco, or coffee, depending on the origin of the beans.

Milk chocolate #

Milk chocolate adds milk powder or condensed milk to the cocoa, which makes it sweeter, softer, and paler than dark chocolate. Most milk chocolate sits between roughly 20% and 40% cocoa.

The milk both rounds off the bitterness and lowers the melting point, which is why milk chocolate feels creamier on the tongue.

White chocolate #

White chocolate is made from cocoa butter, sugar, and milk, but no cocoa solids, which is why it is ivory rather than brown and tastes of cream and vanilla instead of cocoa.

Because it has no cocoa solids, some purists argue it is not really chocolate at all, though it behaves like chocolate when melted.

Ruby chocolate #

Ruby chocolate is the newest type, introduced in 2017. Its natural pink colour and tart, berry-like flavour come from specially processed ruby cocoa beans, with no berries or colouring added.

It is often described as a fourth type alongside dark, milk, and white chocolate.
